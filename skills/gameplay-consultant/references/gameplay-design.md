# Gameplay Design Reference

## Diagnose The Loop

Describe the loop as a sequence the player actually experiences:

1. goal or desire
2. available verbs and information
3. meaningful choice under pressure
4. action and immediate feedback
5. consequence, reward, or loss
6. changed possibility that creates the next goal

If a step lacks a player decision, consequence, or reason to continue, do not hide it behind more content. Fix the missing link first.

## Mechanics And Feedback

- Define the decision the mechanic creates, not only its input and output.
- Make cause, effect, state, and success legible in the available time and sensory channel. Test visual, audio, haptic, and textual feedback against the target platform.
- Separate challenge from opacity. Players can fail a hard task fairly when the rule, feedback, and recovery path are readable.
- Add depth through interacting decisions, timing, position, information, and opportunity cost before adding a long list of isolated verbs.

## Progression And Economy

- State the fantasy of advancement and the player behavior it should reward.
- Map resource sources, sinks, caps, conversion rates, loss conditions, and exploit paths.
- Check short-term reward against long-term goals. A reward that removes future decisions can flatten the loop.
- Include a recovery path after a bad run or mistake unless the game deliberately makes irreversible loss part of its promise.

## Difficulty And Accessibility

- Identify the real skill being tested: recognition, planning, execution, timing, knowledge, coordination, or endurance.
- Increase one form of pressure at a time when diagnosing a difficulty spike; otherwise the cause is not observable.
- Preserve the intended challenge while offering readable controls, remapping, captions, reduced-motion options, color-independent state cues, and alternatives to rapid repeated input where appropriate.
- Do not describe accessibility as an optional polish pass. It affects who can understand, control, and enjoy the loop.

## Playtest Plan

- Recruit players close to the target audience and separate new-player observations from expert feedback.
- Give a task and observe behavior before explaining the design. Record where players hesitate, misread, repeat unproductive actions, or abandon a goal.
- Ask about expectations and decisions after the session, not leading questions about whether a proposed feature sounds good.
- Define a falsifiable hypothesis, a narrow change, an observation method, a threshold, and a follow-up decision before the test.
