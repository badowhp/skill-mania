# Delivery Controls

Use these field sets to build a small, coherent control system. Prefer one maintained source over several documents that repeat and contradict each other.

## Minimum Viable Control Set

Every project needs:

1. an outcome and completion definition
2. a named sponsor, project manager, and decision path
3. owned deliverables or work
4. a forecast with milestones and dependencies
5. risks, current issues, and decisions
6. a dated status and next review
7. acceptance, transition, and closure ownership

Add cost, resource, procurement, quality, compliance, change, and assurance controls when their absence creates a real decision or delivery risk.

## Charter

Record:

- project identifier, version, status, and date
- problem or opportunity and expected value
- measurable outcomes and success conditions
- scope, non-scope, deliverables, and completion events
- sponsor, project manager, team, stakeholders, and decision rights
- deadline, budget or capacity envelope, constraints, and assumptions
- delivery approach and tailoring rationale
- major milestones, dependencies, and initial risks
- acceptance authority, transition owner, and benefits owner
- approval evidence and next gate

Do not use vague goals such as "improve efficiency" without a measure, target, time horizon, and owner.

## Delivery Plan

Choose the planning unit that matches the work:

- Use deliverables and work packages when scope is sufficiently knowable and dependencies dominate.
- Use an ordered backlog and short feedback cycles when solution uncertainty dominates.
- Use both in a hybrid project: project milestones and governance outside, iterative discovery and delivery inside.

For each planning item, keep:

- stable identifier and description
- owner
- output and acceptance criteria
- estimate or range and confidence
- predecessors, external dependencies, and decision lead time
- planned, forecast, and actual dates
- status and evidence

Show constraints and resource contention. A task list without dependency logic is not a reliable schedule.

## RAID and Decision Records

Keep distinct records:

| Record | Meaning | Minimum fields |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Risk | uncertain event that may affect objectives | cause, event, effect, probability, impact, proximity, response, contingency, trigger, owner, review date |
| Assumption | planning belief not yet verified | statement, basis, validation method, owner, due date, consequence if false |
| Issue | condition already affecting the project | evidence, impact, containment, resolution, owner, due date, escalation |
| Dependency | required input, decision, resource, or external output | provider, receiver, needed-by date, commitment, confidence, fallback, owner |
| Decision | authoritative choice | question, options, impact, recommendation, owner, needed-by date, outcome, rationale, date |
| Change | proposed alteration to an approved baseline | request, reason, value, scope/schedule/cost/resource/risk impact, options, approver, decision, baseline updates |

Do not convert every task into an issue or every uncertainty into a high risk. Rank by effect on objectives and decision urgency.

## Status Report

Use this order:

1. as-of date, reporting period, overall status, confidence, and threshold basis
2. executive summary: what changed, why it matters, and the next decision
3. outcome and deliverable status
4. milestone baseline, forecast, actual, variance, and confidence
5. resource and budget baseline, actual, forecast, and variance where applicable
6. top risks, current issues, dependencies, decisions, and changes
7. actions with owner and due date
8. decisions or support needed, decision owner, and needed-by date
9. next review point

Use source dates. If cost, completion, or capacity data is unavailable, say `unknown` and state the decision risk; do not infer green.

## Change Control

1. Log the request without changing the baseline.
2. Check the reason and expected value.
3. Assess impact across outcome, scope, quality, schedule, cost, resources, risk, contracts, operations, and benefits.
4. Compare at least the credible options, including defer or reject.
5. Obtain a decision from the authorised owner.
6. Update affected baselines, plans, logs, contracts, and communications.
7. Verify the change at the next control point.

Emergency action may contain harm before formal approval, but label the authority, time limit, follow-up decision, and restoration or forward-fix path.

## Recovery

When a project is red:

1. Freeze optimistic reporting and establish an evidence date.
2. Identify the failing objective, tolerance, root cause, and immediate containment.
3. Reforecast remaining work from current state, dependencies, capacity, and uncertainty.
4. Develop decision-ready options such as reduce scope, add capacity, resequence, extend time, change approach, pause, or stop.
5. Show value, cost, schedule, resource, risk, and contractual impact for each option.
6. Obtain a sponsor decision and record it.
7. Rebaseline only the approved path.
8. Increase review frequency until leading indicators stabilise.

Do not label a longer task list a recovery plan. A recovery plan changes the conditions that caused the miss.

## Acceptance, Transition, and Closure

Before closure, verify:

- each deliverable has acceptance evidence and an authorised accepter
- operational, support, data, security, training, and documentation handoffs have owners
- open defects, risks, claims, invoices, contracts, and residual actions have disposition
- financial and resource records are reconciled
- lessons name a reusable change, owner, and destination
- benefits have a measure, baseline, owner, and post-project review date
- the sponsor or authorised body has approved closure

Distinguish delivered, accepted, transitioned, adopted, and beneficial. They are different states.

## Escalation Design

Define thresholds before they are crossed. Typical thresholds cover:

- forecast milestone or final-date variance
- cost or capacity tolerance
- quality or acceptance failure
- unresolved high exposure
- critical dependency confidence
- legal, security, privacy, safety, or contractual breach
- benefit erosion or loss of strategic fit

An escalation should contain evidence, impact, options, recommendation, decision owner, and needed-by date.
