## Priority Order
Diagnose and revise in this order unless the user explicitly asks for line editing only:

## Story Engine
Identify what keeps the reader turning pages:

- a mystery to solve
- a threat to survive
- a transformation to witness
- a relationship to root for or fear
- a comic or philosophical voice worth following

If the engine is unclear, name the strongest available one and recommend how to foreground it earlier.

Check:

- what question the opening creates
- what makes the middle deepen rather than merely delay
- what promise the climax fulfills
- whether the ending feels inevitable in hindsight but not obvious in advance

When the user has not specified a framework, default to these heuristics:

- Every scene should contain pressure.
- Exposition should either create anticipation or collide with immediate stakes.
- Character voice should differ in rhythm, priorities, and evasions, not only vocabulary.
- Emotional peaks need concrete triggers and visible cost.
- Themes should recur through choices, images, and consequences.
- Endings should close the immediate movement while opening a larger question.
## Character Pressure
For major characters, track:

- surface goal
- deeper need
- fear or false belief
- contradiction
- pressure point
- what changes after each major beat

Weak character work often comes from one of these failures:

- desire is vague
- the cost of failure is abstract
- the character only reacts
- the inner conflict does not affect decisions
- the arc is stated but not dramatized

Recommended fix pattern:

1. Name the decision point.
2. State what the character wants in that moment.
3. Add the force pulling the character the other way.
4. Show the choice through action, speech, or omission.
## Scene Design
Most scenes improve when they have:

- a goal
- resistance
- a turn
- a changed situation by the end

Questions to ask:

- Why now?
- Who wants what?
- What makes the scene impossible to coast through?
- What new information, wound, alliance, danger, or commitment exists at the end?

When expanding thin scenes, do not add generic description first. Add one or more of:

- a harder choice
- a cleaner objective
- a more specific obstacle
- a reversal
- a cost
- a surprising but logical tactic
- an emotionally revealing detail
## Pacing And Reveal Control
For suspense and mystery:

- answers should create better questions
- reveals should force reinterpretation, not only transfer information
- delay is useful only when it increases pressure
- repeated beats with no escalation should be cut or merged

For slower reflective passages:

- anchor the reflection in an immediate trigger
- keep abstract thought tied to image, memory, or decision
- end on movement, not only contemplation
## Dialogue
Good dialogue is not information exchange alone. It is conflict, seduction, concealment, testing, or failed connection in spoken form.

Check:

- what each speaker wants from the exchange
- what each speaker avoids saying
- whether voices differ in pace, syntax, metaphor, and evasiveness
- whether the dialogue changes the power balance

Useful revision moves:

- cut the line that explains the subtext
- let one character answer a different question than the one asked
- replace exposition with a charged concrete detail
- shorten overlong speeches unless theatricality is deliberately the point
- give the last line of the exchange a sting, pivot, or ache
## Worldbuilding
Worldbuilding should alter decisions, not sit beside them.

Prefer:

- concrete consequences of setting rules
- sensory or bureaucratic details that imply the wider world
- institutions, rituals, slang, or infrastructure showing how people live inside the setting

Avoid:

- encyclopedia paragraphs with no active pressure
- familiar genre wallpaper presented as if it were new
- invented terms with no immediate narrative payoff
## Action And Fight Scenes
Action should be understandable, purposeful, and consequential.

Check:

- geography: can the reader track where everyone is?
- intention: what is each side trying to accomplish?
- adaptation: do tactics change as new problems appear?
- consequence: what is lost, damaged, learned, or exposed?

Revision moves:

- orient the space early
- alternate rapid beats with clear pivots
- replace repetitive blows with decisions and tactics
- let injury, terrain, timing, or resource limits reshape the fight
## Theme And Philosophical Material
Theme lands when it is embodied in choices, imagery, and consequence.

When philosophical dialogue feels thin:

- tie it to a fresh wound or temptation
- let speakers want incompatible truths
- use the idea to corner a decision
- keep at least one line emotionally naked or specific

Avoid speeches that could be moved anywhere in the book without changing meaning.
## Voice And Prose
At line level, preserve what is vivid and particular.

Strengthen prose by:

- replacing abstract labels with precise images or actions
- varying sentence length for rhythm
- cutting redundancy
- sharpening verbs before adding adjectives
- keeping metaphors coherent and earned

For comic or strange fiction, protect the tonal signature. The goal is not "cleaner" at any cost. The goal is clearer force.

Voice protection rules:

- Keep distinctive phrasing when it is working.
- Do not over-normalize into bland business English.
- Preserve deliberate informality, eccentricity, or stylistic texture when it serves the work.
- If a passage is broken but memorable, repair it rather than replacing it wholesale.
- Preserve linguistic identity. If the manuscript is in German, keep the prose genuinely German rather than pseudo-translated English with German words.
- Do not drain wit, bite, weirdness, or musicality in the name of "clarity."
## Revision Passes
Run revisions in passes instead of trying to fix everything at once:

1. Plot and structure pass
2. Character and motivation pass
3. Scene tension pass
4. Dialogue pass
5. Prose pass
6. Continuity and callback pass
7. Proofread and publishing pass
## Production-Ready Novel Checklist
Before calling a novel production-ready, confirm:

- the opening establishes genre, tone, and reader promise quickly
- the midpoint changes the story rather than repeating setup energy
- the final act pays off the book's core question and motif
- important secondary characters affect outcomes instead of decorating scenes
- chapter endings create forward pull
- terminology, timeline, and power rules stay consistent
- real-world placeholders or accidental references are removed if the setting requires it
- packaging matches the actual reading experience
## Failure Modes To Avoid
- generic "elevated" prose that loses the author's identity
- plot summary passed off as scene writing
- dialogue that explains rather than competes
- ornamental worldbuilding that pauses momentum
- fake profundity without emotional grounding
- action scenes with motion but no tactical logic
- notes that identify problems without suggesting a practical fix
